## Abstract [PDF] (/pdfs/Algo_Econ_Final_Project.pdf)
Instructed by Professor Mattheus X.V Feirrera. University course enrollment systems often suffer from inefficiencies, including misallocation of limited
seats, strategic behavior under priority-based systems, and limited ability for students to express preference
intensity. In this paper, we propose and study an auction-based mechanism for course allocation which models
enrollment as a resource allocation problem with heterogeneous student valuations. We previously proposed
the use of combinatorial auctions for capturing complex preferences, but in this paper, we focus on a simpler
and more practical use of a simultaneous second-price, sealed-bid (SPSB) framework where students submit
independent bids for individual courses subject to budget constraints.
We analyze the efficiency, incentive properties, and implementability of this mechanism, comparing it
to both existing enrollment systems as well as select alternatives such as lottery-based and random serial
dictatorship approaches. Our model examines how SPSB auctions can improve allocative efficiency while
maintaining tractability and usability in real-world academic settings.
## Future Considerations

Major-size externality may be an area of further research, in addition to student behavior in response to this system. While we believe 
our hybrid SPSB design preserves tractability and can be easily understood by students, actual human response is much less certain.
